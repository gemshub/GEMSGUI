Surface complexation on mineral-water interfaces can be calculated in GEMS in a way similar to non-ideal solid solutions, with account for surface heterogeneity, multi-site adsorption and surface site saturation.

It is assumed that ideal surface binding of ions from bulk aqueous electrolyte at low fractional surface coverage occurs in water monolayer in a "linear adsorption region" where the surface density (concentration) of adsorbed ions increases proportional to the ion activity in the bulk solution. Electrostatic effects on charged surface cause the surface complex concentration differ from the ideal one due to Coulombic correction terms that depend on the relative surface plane potentials, calculated from an electrostatic model of choice (CCM, DLM, TLM, XTLM, BSM, XBSM). Electrostatic corrections can also be ignored if non-electrostatic model (NEM) is selected for a given surface type on a given sorbent phase.
 
In GEMS, one can have several sorption phases (with known specific surface area of the sorbent) with up to six different surface types on each one. Each surface type can have different EDL model and different maximum site density acting for a (non)competitive adsorption of several surface species. The site saturation effects are accounted for by  using the surface activity terms (SAT) - activity-coefficient-like expressions derived from the Langmuir isotherm.

Details about mathematical representation of GEM SCMs and calculation of Coulombic terms and SAT are given in the SCM-corrections.pdf document (see /program/doc/pdf directory or GEMS-PSI web page) and references therein.
